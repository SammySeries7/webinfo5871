Clustering


What clustering does and how to use it to gain information


Clustering is an unsupervised learning technique that groups similar data points together based on their similarity or distance from one another. The goal of clustering is to divide the data into meaningful subgroups, or clusters, such that data points within a cluster are more similar to each other than to data points in other clusters.


This technique can be used to identify the main topics and subtopics within the unstructured dataset like texts or tweets. It also helps to reduce the complexity of the words by aggregating the dataset into several topics. The technique also helps to reveal the opaque relationships between words and generate insight in the data.


In addition, several clustering algorithms are compared to each other in order to understand pros and cons in each method. The methods are K-Means clustering, and Hierarchical clustering. There is no exact solution on how to select methods, so it is a good chance to explore and experience these techniques with this text dataset. After the clusters are assigned into the data, the common word extraction will be applied to understand the main topics in each cluster and assess how well the clustering algorithms are performed. Besides, a visualization such as Silhouette analysis plot and dendrogram will be employed to gain insight into each cluster from the methods.

Results and Discussion


K-Means


In this project, several clusters have been tried in the K-Means model, which are 5, 10, 25 and 50. These numbers have been selected from the elbow plot and Silhouette score.


The elbow plot of K-Means is the plot between the sum of squared distance between each point and the centroid in a cluster and the number of clusters. By visualizing the mentioned distance as a function of the number of clusters, the elbow plot can help practitioners to choose the number of clusters that balances the trade-off between having too few clusters (underfitting) and too many clusters (overfitting).


The Silhouette score is a measure of the quality of the solution obtained from a clustering algorithm, such as K-Means. It provides a measure of how well each sample has been assigned to its own cluster compared to other clusters. The Silhouette score ranges from -1 to 1, where a higher score indicates a better-quality clustering.


The Silhouette score is computed for each sample by comparing the similarity of a sample to its own cluster compared to other clusters. Specifically, for each sample, two values are computed: the average similarity of the sample to all other samples in its own cluster, and the average similarity of the sample to all other samples in the nearest neighboring cluster. The Silhouette score for a sample is then given by the difference between these two values, divided by the greater of the two values. The Silhouette score is plotted with the number of clusters as the same as the elbow plot, so it can help interpreters to select the suitable cluster number for the model.

In the case of k=25, silhouette score plot informs that there are many clusters that cannot differentiate from each other, which can be seen from the cluster’s score that is below zero. In addition, when the most common words are extracted from the different K-Mean models, it is noticeable that the more k-value model tends to have the similar word patterns in many clusters.

Conclusions. What did you learn that pertains to your topic?


According to the results, K-Means tends to outperform hierarchical clustering. The way to assess the performance is to extract the words from each cluster to determine the main topic, which K-Means results are easily understandable. Regarding the number of clusters for the K-Means, both Silhouette score and elbow method remain unstable in spite of high k-value around 50. In addition, the Silhouette scores from k=5 to k=50 are not much different. Therefore, the word extraction, Silhouette analysis, and score must be considered selecting the suitable number of clusters together, which k equal to 5 is proper in this dataset. In hierarchical clustering, the k equal to 5 is still preferable. According to the dendrogram, the k more than 5 is not likely to separate the topics between clusters. The main five topics are EVs’ news, EV’s price, charging infrastructure, environmental impacts, and Elon Musk.
